Assigning Teams

Time Limit: 2 seconds

Arranging a board game night sounds like it would be a lot of run, right? If only your friends actually enjoyed each others’ company.

For the games you want to play, you’ll need to divide people in to two teams. The sizes of the teams don’t necessarily matter, just that both teams have at least one person. Normally this would be easy, but your “friends” don’t get along. After you proposed this idea, many of your friends submitted complaints. Bobby told you “I can’t be on a team with Billy! He stole my pringles!”. Similarly, Sally complained, “I can’t be on a team with Harry, we broke up only 3 years ago!”.

Despite the issues, you still want to hold your game night. You make a long list of the complaints and decide that you will attempt to build two teams that satisfy everyone’s demands. Given a complaint made between person aa and person bb, you will try to put person aa on a different team than person bb.

Write a program to assist in this task. Given NN people (numbered 0...N−10...N−1), and a list of pairs (i,j)(i,j) such that i,ji,j cannot be on the same team, can you construct two teams?

Input

Input will begin with one line containing two integers N(2≤N≤5000)N(2≤N≤5000) E(1≤E≤20,000)E(1≤E≤20,000), denoting the number of people and the number of issues respectively.

EE lines will follow, each of consisting of two integers i,j(0≤i,j<N)i,j(0≤i,j<N) denoting that person ii and person jj do not want to be on the same team.

Output

Output a single line containing either the word “yes” or the word “no”. Output “yes” if it is possible to create two teams given the constraints, or “no” otherwise.

Friend Groups

Time Limit: 2 seconds

Let’s define a friend group as a number of people who can be connected by mutual friends. For example, take four people: Alice, Bob, Carter, and David. Say that Alice and Bob are friends; Carter and David are friends; and Bob and Carter are friends. Then we can say that Alice, Bob, Carter, and David form a friend group of four people. However, if Bob and Carter were not friends, then there would be two distinct friend groups: Alice and Bob would be separate from Carter and David.

Given a list of people and their friendships, can you determine the number of distinct friend groups?

A person with no friends is considered to be in their own friend group.

Input

Input will begin with a line containing two integers N(1≤N≤5000)N(1≤N≤5000), and F(1≤F≤50000)F(1≤F≤50000) denoting the number of people and the number of “friendship” pairs respectively. The following line will contain NN names (consisting of only upper-case and lower-case characters) each separated by a single space. FF lines will follow, all of the form aa bb, with aa, bb being names specified in the second line, denoting that aa and bb are friends.

Output

Output a single number on a single line denoting the number of distinct friend groups.

Kickball! (Hard)

Time Limit: 4 seconds

To improve working relationships, your employer has decided to arrange an employee kickball game! Kickball is played between two teams (red and blue) of players and is very similar to baseball.

Unfortunately, many of your coworkers have trouble getting along. Despite the game being arranged to bring them closer together, they have submitted a long list of players that, for various reasons, they do not want on their team. To keep everyone happy, you’ve been assigned the job of arranging the teams such that no two players who hate each other are on the same team.

While working around employee’s animosity, you also want to keep the game fair by making the teams as balanced as possible. Nobody at your office really even knows how to play kickball, so you’ll just keep them balanced by the number of players on each team.
